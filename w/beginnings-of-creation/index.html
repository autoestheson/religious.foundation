The Beginnings of Creation

I'm pleased to write about a new project of mine, Poiesis: a simple operating system with highly undefined goals.

When I say simple, I mean simple — Poiesis is, at the moment, a bootloader, and going forward, it is likely not to become too much more. My vision for it ranges anywhere from TripOS, which could be described as "a more advanced DOS," to a step above some early version of MacOS. Although I realize I will almost certainly never be able to truly use Poiesis as a replacement for, say, Linux, I'm trying to design something I would like to use. Some things in modern operating systems that really turn me off include: inconsistent or misdirected design ideals, incomplete functionality, distrust in the user, indulgence, and things of such ilk. The thing is, these aren't too hard to avoid — as long as you have a defined-enough end-goal and stop there, you almost certianly will not go wrong. Thus is my plan for Poiesis.

That being said, even as I write this, my mind is shifting. See, Poiesis is written for a 32-bit x86 platform. As I said earlier, I can't expect to seriously use it — especially considering the fact that its platform is so outdated as to be impossible to run on the bare metal of my computer. "But with this in mind," I often find myself thinking, "why should I bother writing for such a complex architecture?" For the uninitiated, the x86 architecture is so painfully complex to write an operating system for that it's almost made me quit several times over. Even with an ongoing project I resort to compromises so as to not overwork myself; for example, I use a primitive method of querying memory rather than using the more complex standard technique. Given the early stage of the project, I'm considering writing for a different architecture: perhaps RISC-V, or a hypothetical homebrew architecture. Considering my goal of learning the inner workings of machines, I wonder — would I be best served forcing myself to learn the x86 architecture, or would it be better to learn with a hypothetical architecture?
