The Santa Fe Dialect of Ancient Greek

home-sfg-ag

Uh-oh. It looks like you're having a conversation with a speaker of the peculiar Santa Fe Dialect of Ancient Greek!

Never heard of it? That makes sense — Santa Fe doesn't have many active Ancient-Greek-speaking communities, but it does have more than most places (source: wild conjecture) — so it's developed a distinct yet generally unknown system of consonants and vowels.

In all seriousness, this isn't actually a dialect, and the people who speak it tend to be some of the more dedicated and studious members of the Santa Fe Greek Speaking Community. What it really is is a set of hypercorrections caused by the influence of American English and a general lack of access to precise definitions of any reconstructed historical pronunciations.

The Weird Things

Let's be real. Almost nobody speaks Ancient Greek with an accent even close to what we've reconstructed, especially not native English speakers. Ancient Greek had a pitch accent system and many properties, like vowel length and consonant gemination, that we're simply not used to. It's why my accent isn't perfect. So I'm not going to make fun of people for things that are perfectly human mistakes. I'm only going to focus on the worst and most jarring offenders, in my opinion.

Stop Consonant Shenanigans

Reconstructed Pronunciation

Reconstructed pronunciations of Ancient Greek describe a three-way distinction in stop consonants: voiced, voiceless unaspirated, and voiceless aspirated. For example, γάμμα, κάππα, and χεῖ would have been pronounced as /g/, /k/, and /kʰ/, respectively.

On the other hand, most English accents have a two-way distinction: voiced and voiceless. In this system, aspiration is allophonic, with the aspirated /kʰ/ appearing word- and stressed-syllable-initially, and the unaspirated /k/ appearing in other places. There's a bit more too it, but that's the gist of it. I bring this up because it adds a very reasonable hurdle to an accurate Greek pronunciation. Unless you learn to accurately distinguish aspiration, you're not going to be able to get that three-way distinction found in Ancient Greek.

This conundrum is why most alternative pronunciations simply pronounce voiceless unaspirated consonants as, well, voiceless unaspirated, and voiceless aspirated as voiceless fricatives with a roughly similar place of articulation. So for most English speakers, γάμμα, κάππα, and χεῖ would be pronounced as /g/, /k/, and /x/ or /χ/. While ideally κάππα should be pronounced only as [k], it's often pronounced as /kʰ/ as well because (a) it's easier for English speakers and (b) aspiration is an otherwise empty niche, so one could argue there's no reason not to fill it.

Santa Fe Pronunciation

For voiced and voiceless unaspirated stop consonants, the Santa Fe Dialect pronounces them about as above, generally aspirating voiceless consonants in alignment with English phonotactics relating to aspiration. Therefore, γάμμα and κάππα are pronounced /g/ and /k/ or /kʰ/.

Likewise, voiceless aspirated stop consonants are generally pronounced as their respective fricatives — with one key exception. Look at the pronunciation of digraphs inherited into English through Greek loanwords: while "ph" and "th" are pronounced as if they were "f" and, well, "th", "ch" is pronounced as if it were "k". So... logically speaking, of course, this indicates that historically, χεῖ was pronounced as a "k"-sound, right? But that can't be right — most Foreign Languages pronounce their stop consonants without aspiration, and Ancient Greek is a Foreign Language, so χεῖ must be pronounced the Foreign Way.

So γάμμα, κάππα, and χεῖ are pronounced as /g/, /k/ or /kʰ/, and /k/. Therefore, the aspirated has become strictly unaspirated, while the unaspirated has gained an aspirated allophone. At least they haven't touched my voiced consonants :)

Fiddling with the Spiritus Asper

Reconstructed Pronunciation

It's almost silly to call it "reconstructed" because Ancient Greek grammarians told us pretty clearly about how it's pronounced.

In any case, "Spiritus Asper" is a fancy way of saying "the letter H". Because it only occured word-initially, its presence or absence at the beginning of a word was indicated by a smooth- or rough-breathing diacritic. For example, look at the word "ῠ̔πέρ". Its first letter, ῠ̔, has the rough-breathing diacritic (looks like an apostrophe), meaning that it begins with an H: pronounced /hyper/, not /yper/.

Santa Fe Pronunciation

Did you notice how that rough-breathing diacritic looks just like an apostrophe? I hope so, because I put it in parentheses. Anyways, as it turns out, because it doesn't look like other letters, that means that you actually don't need to pronounce it. That's right. It's okay! (It's not.)

This, compounded with a general American-English induced inability to reproduce the sounds /y/ and /r/, means that the word "ῠ̔πέρ" is often pronounced in the Santa Fe Dialect as /uːpɚ/ (like, "ooper").

Long Vowels

Reconstructed Pronunciation

The Greek orthography has seven vowel letters: α, ε, η, ι ο, ω, and υ, and we generally agree that the difference between ε/η and ο/ω is primarily length and secondarily openness. So while ε is /e/, η is /ɛː/, and likewise, while ο is /o/, ω is /ɔː/. Vowels are definitely the hardest thing to get right about any sort of pronunciation, especially from a natively English perspective, but it's good to at least know the basic idea.

Santa Fe Pronunciation

Vowel length doesn't really exist in American English, so we don't use it for Greek, either. That'd be too hard. However, it's not that we don't have long vowels, it's just that we can't so easily distinguish between long and short vowels. So speakers of the Santa Fe Dialect have a somewhat random vowel length, with a tendency towards longer vowels — perhaps as a hypercorrection.

As a genuine hypercorrection, there is a notable merger between ἄλφα and ὂ μικρόν. I don't know the exact reason for this, but I theorize that it's a misunderstanding of educational materials. Popular textbooks in the Santa Fe area teach ὦ μέγα as sounding like "go", while ὂ μικρόν sounds like "pot" or German "Gott". And here's our clue: in American English, "pot" has the vowel /ɑ/, which represents the general "ah" sound. But /ɑ/ is not the sound of "Gott". "Gott" has an /ɔ/ sound, which helps us remember that these popular textbooks weren't written for Americans. They were written for speakers of the Received Pronunciation of English, where "pot" has the short /ɒ/ vowel, as opposed to the long /ɑː/ vowel found in words like "bath" or "palm". And this is where we find our merger. Short ἄλφα is taught as sounding like "cup" while long ἄλφα is taught as sounding like "father". In Received Pronunciation, I believe these vowels would be pronounced as /ʌ/ and /ɑː/, leaving ὂ μικρόν distinct as /ɒ/, while in American English, they are pronounced respectively as /ə/, /ɑ/, and /ɑ/. Further, because /ə/ is so obviously not "A-like", speakers shift it to /ɑ/, while /ɑ/ is definitely "O-like" — it's used in the standard American English pronunciation of "ɑmɪkɹɑn" — so we are left with a 3-way merger of short and long ἄλφαs and short ὂ[ μικρόν]s.

I imagine that the original choice of "cup", "father", "pot", and "go" was an attempt at replicating the length/openness changes as best as possible respecting the limitations of English phonology. Considering the vowels they originally meant to replicate were /a aː o ɔː/, /ʌ ɑː ɒ əʊ/ isn't too far off. Length is definitely preserved and openness is approximately preserved. I just wish there were a more concrete way of teaching these things that could avoid such a jarring /ɑ ɑ ɑ oʊ/!
