Ἡληνιγγλισς

Introduction

So you're writing stuff in English when you realize "This is fucked!"

And boom, just like that: you're now a part of a long line of amateur linguists who want to "fix" English spelling.

That was once me. I thought the Latin script could accomodate my needs. Unfortunately, this is probably (I say probably because I'm not an expert) one of those situations where there are too many goals and they conflict with one another in too many ways to be able to use all of them.

This, my dear reader, is where I had a most exquisite realization; namely, that the Latin script has too many of the wrong things (namely, consonant letters) and too few of the right things (namely, vowels) for my Anglic needs. Obviously, the only solution for any self-respecting Hellenicist such as myself is to revert to the Polytonic Greek Orthography! This HTML file serves to document and extoll the virtues of my new system of writing which I affectionately call "Helleninglish," or, typed Hellenically, "Ἡληνιγγλισς."

The System

My system is in my opinion fairly straightforward. Now, I've read enough historical linguistic works to know that "fairly straightforward" means "I use a weird system," and it's true! My tables are not arranged in the typical way and I'm sure once you understand how they're arranged you'll agree that really there was no other way I could arrange them.

For all the letters which I could just easily match up with English phonemes — for example, m and μ — I just did that.

The complex ones are where I had to make hard decisions. Ancient Greek represents gemination with doubled letters, while my English has no real gemination, so that gave me a lot of digraphs which I didn't need. For example, β for b, and ββ for v. I sometimes like to think that English doesn't really have phonemic affricates and they're actually just t-sh and d-zh clusters, but I didn't like the idea of having to write τ-σς or δ-ζζ when I can write the same thing with two letters in the Latin script, so I found a two letter solution in illegal voicing combinations. I can't think of anywhere t-z or d-s occur without assimilating in voice, (to ts or dz) so I ended up with τζ for ch or tsh.

Of course, I do have to mention that these decisions make my system, in some senses, inconsistent: why does doubling β change its manner while doubling ς changes its place and doubling γ changes both? Of course I don't actually care about this argument because in my head it is perfectly consistent: doubling letters changes something and in the same way you are expected to know what letters mean you are also expected to know what doubled letters mean. I just felt the need to include this paragraph because in high school I was graded on the "complexity" of my arguments.

Lastly there is the question of h and ch/kh: the obvious choice is to assign h to the rough breathing mark and ch/kh to χ, and so I did that.

Don't ask about W and Y — they weren't important to the Romans, they weren't important to the Greeks, and they weren't important to me.

English vowels are supremely strange, especially in relation to orthography and where in my head I sense connections between them. So this system is essentially a compromise between "where the vowels feel" and "where the vowels are" and that's especially weird because, if it's not obvious, the above table is not at all laid out according to where the vowels really are. If you simply ignore that minor detail, you can hopefully see why the Greek system of writing vowels is actually a perfect fit for my system of speaking vowels!

First genius feature: iota subscript. English diphthongs are all over the place and I'd hate to have to write two letters for all of this stuff! So I'm taking advantage of this neat feature to write fewer letters.

Second genius feature or first terrible mistake: omicron/omega distinction. My analysis is lopsided, with more A-sounds than O-sounds, so I simply shifted things around a little bit. α on its own is æ not a, ο on its own is a not ɔ, and then also (terrible mistake territory) I prioritized iota subscript so you see ω where you might otherwise expect ο. This also means that one of three English diphthongs that end in an "υ" sound can be represented simply by ω, the "Long O" — yeet!

Third genius feature: Greek "diphthongs". For a significant chunk of time, ει and ου weren't pronounced as "ei" and "ou" (equivalent to English "Long A" and "Long O"), they were pronounced as "ee" and "oo". Or at least that's what I keep getting told! Anyways, because I used ε as a schwa and ο as a "Short O", there's nowhere the sequences ει and ου could be found to actually mean ε-ι or ο-υ. So I was safe in using them for what would otherwise have to be written as ιι and υυ, which I think look gross.
